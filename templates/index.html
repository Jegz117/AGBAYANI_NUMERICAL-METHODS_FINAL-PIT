Core Formula

\[P_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} y_kL_k(x)\] \[L_k(x)=\prod_{\substack{j=0\\j\ne k}}^{n}\frac{x-x_j}{x_k-x_j}\]

Use this method for equal or unequal spacing, provided all x-values are distinct.

Mathematical Discussion

What is Lagrange Interpolation?

Lagrange interpolation is a numerical method used to estimate an unknown value of a function from a set of known data points. Given several ordered pairs, the method constructs one interpolating polynomial that passes exactly through every provided point. This polynomial can then be evaluated at a chosen target value of x.

This method is useful for both equally spaced and unequally spaced data. Unlike forward, backward, or central difference formulas, Lagrange interpolation does not require a difference table. The only major requirement is that all x-values must be distinct, because repeated x-values would make a basis denominator equal to zero.

General Form

\[ P_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n} y_k L_k(x) \]

Here, \(P_n(x)\) is the interpolating polynomial, \(y_k\) is the known function value, and \(L_k(x)\) is the kth Lagrange basis polynomial.

1. Basis Polynomial

\[ L_k(x)=\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j\ne k}}^{n} \frac{x-x_j}{x_k-x_j} \]

Each basis polynomial is built by multiplying fractions that compare the target value \(x\) with every other data point. The condition \(j\ne k\) means the kth point is skipped in its own product.

2. Interpolation Property

\[ L_k(x_j)= \begin{cases} 1, & j=k \\ 0, & j\ne k \end{cases} \]

A basis polynomial becomes 1 at its own x-value and 0 at the other x-values. This property forces the final polynomial to pass through all given data points.

3. Final Approximation

\[ \begin{aligned} P_n(x) &= y_0L_0(x)+y_1L_1(x)+\cdots \\ &\quad +y_nL_n(x) \end{aligned} \]

After computing each basis value, each one is multiplied by its matching y-value. The sum of all contributions gives the estimated result.

Procedure Used by the Calculator

  1. Enter the data points \((x_0,y_0),(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)\).
  2. Choose the target value of x where the polynomial will be evaluated.
  3. Compute every basis polynomial \(L_k(x)\) using the Lagrange basis formula.
  4. Multiply each basis value by its matching y-value to get each contribution.
  5. Add all contributions to obtain \(P_n(x)\), the interpolated value.

Any spacing

The x-values may be equally or unequally spaced, as long as every x-value is distinct.

Basis logic

Each basis polynomial becomes 1 at its own point and 0 at all other data points.

Best for small data sets

The method is clear and effective for small n, but high-degree curves may oscillate.

Two Fully Worked Examples

Example 1

Estimate y at x = 1.5

Given: (0, 2), (1, 3), (2, 6)

Formula

\[P_2(x)=y_0L_0(x)+y_1L_1(x)+y_2L_2(x)\]

Substitute and evaluate the basis polynomials

\[L_0(1.5)=\frac{(1.5-1)(1.5-2)}{(0-1)(0-2)}=\frac{-0.25}{2}=-0.1250\]

\[L_1(1.5)=\frac{(1.5-0)(1.5-2)}{(1-0)(1-2)}=\frac{-0.75}{-1}=0.7500\]

\[L_2(1.5)=\frac{(1.5-0)(1.5-1)}{(2-0)(2-1)}=\frac{0.75}{2}=0.3750\]

Final substitution

\[P_2(1.5)=2(-0.1250)+3(0.7500)+6(0.3750)\]

Final answer

\[\boxed{P_2(1.5)=4.2500}\]

Example 2

Estimate y at x = 1

Given: (-1, 4), (0, 1), (2, 3), (3, 10)

Formula

\[P_3(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{3}y_kL_k(x)\]

Substitute and evaluate the basis polynomials

\[L_0(1)=\frac{(1-0)(1-2)(1-3)}{(-1-0)(-1-2)(-1-3)}=\frac{2}{-12}=-0.1667\]

\[L_1(1)=\frac{(1-(-1))(1-2)(1-3)}{(0-(-1))(0-2)(0-3)}=\frac{4}{6}=0.6667\]

\[L_2(1)=\frac{(1-(-1))(1-0)(1-3)}{(2-(-1))(2-0)(2-3)}=\frac{-4}{-6}=0.6667\]

\[L_3(1)=\frac{(1-(-1))(1-0)(1-2)}{(3-(-1))(3-0)(3-2)}=\frac{-2}{12}=-0.1667\]

Final substitution

\[P_3(1)=4(-0.1667)+1(0.6667)+3(0.6667)+10(-0.1667)\]

Final answer

\[\boxed{P_3(1)=0.3333}\]
